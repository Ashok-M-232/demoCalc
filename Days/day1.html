SOC Deployment Models ‚Äî Explained

A Security Operations Center (SOC) can be deployed in different ways depending on the organization‚Äôs size, resources, and security strategy. The two primary deployment models are:

1Ô∏è‚É£ MSSP ‚Äî Managed Security Service Provider

  • Definition: MSSP is a third-party service provider that monitors, manages, and responds to security threats on behalf of an organization.
  • Key Features:
    • 24/7 monitoring and alerting
    • Threat detection and response handled externally
    • Security expertise without hiring a full internal team
    • Often includes SIEM as a service, managed firewalls, and incident response
  • Advantages:
    • Cost-effective for small-to-medium organizations
    • Access to advanced security tools and expertise
    • Reduced operational overhead for internal staff
  • Considerations:
    • Less control over data and operations
    • Dependent on service-level agreements (SLAs)

2Ô∏è‚É£ In-House SOC

  • Definition: An internal team fully owned and operated by the organization to handle all security operations.
  • Key Features:
    • Complete control over monitoring, investigation, and response
    • Team works directly with internal systems, applications, and networks
    • Can integrate closely with IT, DevOps, and compliance teams
  • Advantages:
    • Maximum visibility and control over security events
    • Customized security policies and workflows
    • Faster response times for critical incidents
  • Considerations:
    • High cost to set up and maintain
    • Requires skilled personnel and ongoing training
    • Infrastructure and tool investments are needed

Summary:

Model Ownership Cost Control Expertise
MSSP Third-party Lower Medium Provided externally
In-House SOC Organization Higher High Provided internally

üí° Tip: Some organizations use a hybrid approach, combining internal SOC staff with MSSPs to balance cost, expertise, and control.

Events & Kill Chain ‚Äî Explained

In Cyber Security, monitoring events is critical for detecting and responding to threats. Events can be categorized as:

1Ô∏è‚É£ Normal Event

Normal events represent expected behavior in the system, network, or application. These are routine logs and actions that follow standard operational patterns.

  • Example: User login during office hours
  • Example: Scheduled backups running as planned
  • Example: Regular file access by authorized users

2Ô∏è‚É£ Abnormal Event

Abnormal events are unexpected or suspicious activities that may indicate potential security incidents. These require investigation by the SOC team.

  • Example: Multiple failed login attempts from the same user
  • Example: Unusual access to sensitive files outside office hours
  • Example: Unexpected network traffic spikes

Cyber Kill Chain & MITRE ATT&CK

The Cyber Kill Chain is a framework that describes the stages of a cyber attack. Combined with MITRE ATT&CK, it helps SOC teams understand attacker techniques, tactics, and procedures (TTPs).

Typical Attack Flow:

  • Initial Access ‚Äî Gaining entry into the system (phishing, exploit, stolen credentials)
  • Privilege Escalation ‚Äî Attacker gains higher access rights
  • Lateral Movement ‚Äî Moving across network to find critical assets
  • Exfiltration ‚Äî Stealing data from the organization

Key Takeaways:

  • Normal events help establish a baseline of expected behavior.
  • Abnormal events are early indicators of potential attacks.
  • Understanding the Cyber Kill Chain and MITRE ATT&CK helps SOC teams proactively detect, respond, and prevent attacks.
